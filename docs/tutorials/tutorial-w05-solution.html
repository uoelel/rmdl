2 Mean f0

Fit a regression model to answer the following question:

Do the months spent in Germany modulate the effect of attitude on mean f0?

The model should have the following variables:

  • f0mn as the outcome variable.
  • gender, months_ger and attitude as predictors.
  • Add varying effects as needed.
polite <- read_csv("data/winter2012/polite.csv")

We fit a Bayesian regression model with mean f0 as the outcome variable, using a Gaussian family. As predictors, we include gender, attitude and months spent in Germany, including all interactions. We also let the effect of attitude to vary by subject. (The effects of gender and months in Germany cannot vary by subject because they are within-subject variables).

f0_bm <- brm(
  f0mn ~ gender * attitude * months_ger +
    (attitude | subject),
  family = gaussian,
  data = polite,
  cores = 4,
  seed = my_seed,
  file = "data/cache/f0_bm"
)

The coefficients of interest are attitudepol:months_ger (for females) and genderM:attitudepol:months_ger. The 90% CrI of attitudepol:months_ger is [-0.04, 0.22]: for each month spent in Germany the effect of attitude (polite) changes by -0.04 to +0.22 at 90% probability. While most of CrI is positive, a negative but small interaction could exist. Since the effect of attitude (polite) is negative, the interaction term indicates that the effect of attitude might decrease with time spent in Germany for female speakers. For male speakers, the interaction term genderM:attitudepol:months_ger adds more uncertainty, so it is not wise to make any inference. See Figure 1 for the expected mean f0 based on the model.

plot(
  conditional_effects(f0_bm, "months_ger:attitude", conditions = make_conditions(polite, "gender")),
  points = TRUE
)

3 H1-H2 difference

If you have time, run another regression model to answer the following research question:

Does being a music student modulate the effect of attitude on the H1-H2 difference?

The H1-H2 difference is correlated with breathiness: the higher the difference, the more breathy the voice quality.

We fit a Bayesian regression with H1-H2 difference as the outcome variable, with a Gaussian family. As predictors, the model has attitude, gender and whether the participant is a music student, including all interactions. We let the affect of attitude to vary by participant by including a by-subject varying intercept and varying slope for attitude. Figure 2 shows the expected H1-H2 difference depending on attitude, gender and music student based on the model.

h1h2_bm <- brm(
  H1H2 ~ attitude * musicstudent * gender + (attitude | subject),
  data = polite,
  family = gaussian,
  cores = 4,
  seed = my_seed,
  file = "data/cache/h1h2_bm"
)

To answer the RQ of whether the difference between informal and polite attitude is affected by whether the participant is a music student, we calculate the following estimands:

  • Difference between H1-H2 in informal vs polite in female participants, separately for music students (mus_f) vs non-music students (nomus_f).

  • Difference between H1-H2 in informal vs polite in male participants, separately for music students (mus_m) vs non-music students (nomus_m).

  • Difference between the differences for female and male participants (no_mus_f and no_mus_m). This estimand is the one we need to answer the RQ.

h1h2_draws <- as_draws_df(h1h2_bm, "^b_", regex = TRUE)
h1h2_draws <- h1h2_draws |> 
  mutate(
    nomus_f = b_attitudepol,
    mus_f = b_attitudepol + `b_attitudepol:musicstudentyes`,
    no_mus_f = mus_f - nomus_f,
    nomus_m = b_attitudepol + `b_attitudepol:genderM`,
    mus_m = b_attitudepol + `b_attitudepol:genderM` + `b_attitudepol:musicstudentyes` + `b_attitudepol:musicstudentyes:genderM`,
    no_mus_m = mus_m - nomus_m,
  )

# Pivot for plotting
h1h2_music_diff <- h1h2_draws |> 
  select(no_mus_f, no_mus_m) |> 
  pivot_longer(everything())
Warning: Dropping 'draws_df' class as required metadata was removed.

Figure 3 shows the estimated difference between the by-attitude H1-H2 difference in music vs non-music students, separately for female and male students. There is some indication of a difference for female participants, but not so much for male participants. Let’s calculate 95, 80, and 60% CrI.

h1h2_music_diff |> 
  ggplot(aes(value, fill = name)) +
  geom_vline(xintercept = 0) +
  geom_density(alpha = 0.5)

The 80% CrI [-1.98, -0.22] for female participants suggests a difference of the effect of attitude based on whether the participant is a music student or not, at 80% confidence, although the magnitude of the difference is quite uncertain and if a difference is indeed present it is less than 2 db. For male participants there is a lot of uncertainty.

h1h2_music_diff |> 
  group_by(name) |> 
  summarise(
    ci = list(round(quantile2(value, probs = c(0.05, 0.1, 0.2, 0.8, 0.9, 0.95)), 2))
  ) |> 
  unnest_wider(ci)

In sum, the results are inconclusive as to which being a music student makes a difference in H1-H2 differences between polite and informal speech for male speakers. For female speakers, there might be a slightly greater difference in the H1-H2 difference of polite vs informal speech when they are music students relative to non-music students, but the difference is below 2 db.
